# The Science of Getting Rich (Wallace D. Wattles, 1910)

## Central thesis

Getting rich follows knowable principles, not luck or circumstance. Anyone who
does things in a "Certain Way" (deliberate, faith-driven, gratitude-driven
action) will get rich, because the method, not the person or the trade, is what
produces results.

## Main arguments

- **A creative, not competitive, mindset.** Wealth should be pursued by creating
  new value, not by fighting others for a fixed pool. See
  [[creative-vs-competitive-mind]].
- **Act in the present.** You can only act where you are, now. Do today's work
  efficiently rather than waiting for a better moment. See
  [[acting-in-the-present]].
- **Gratitude and vision.** Holding a clear vision with gratitude keeps you
  aligned toward growth rather than scarcity.
- **Efficient action compounds.** Each act done in the Certain Way is a success
  that makes the next more likely. Overlaps with [[perseverance]].

## Citable points

- "There is a science of getting rich, and it is an exact science, like algebra"
  (opening chapters). Frames wealth as method-driven.
- The distinction between the "competitive mind" and the "creative mind" runs
  through the middle chapters and is the book's most transferable idea.

## Limitations

- Written in a 1910 New Thought register: metaphysical claims ("thinking stuff",
  formless substance) are asserted, not demonstrated. Treat the psychological and
  behavioral advice as the durable core; treat the metaphysics as period framing.
- No empirical evidence; it is a philosophy, not a study.
